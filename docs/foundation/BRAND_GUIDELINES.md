# JOHAI Brand Guidelines

## Authority and status

- **Document type:** Permanent brand architecture
- **Status:** Adopted direction; visual token and asset implementation remains separate work
- **Authority:** Subordinate to applicable law, binding obligations, security, privacy, accessibility, explicit human approval, mandatory database safety, and the [JOHAI Constitution](../constitution/JOHAI_CONSTITUTION.md)
- **Runtime effect:** None
- **Capability effect:** None

The JOHAI brand should make trustworthy intelligence feel calm, prepared, and useful. It must express the [North Star](JOHAI_NORTH_STAR.md), [Product Promise](PRODUCT_PROMISE.md), and [Company Values](COMPANY_VALUES.md) without turning aspiration into a capability claim.

These guidelines define design intent and review rules. They do not create a finished logo package, color-token library, component system, illustration library, production theme, or certified accessible interface.

## Brand idea

> **Intelligence that prepares the work and gives the relationship back to people.**

JOHAI is not the hero of the customer's story. The represented business is. The brand supports that business with quiet competence: it clarifies state, reduces effort, and makes responsibility visible without demanding attention for the software.

## Brand personality

JOHAI is:

- **Prepared:** It anticipates the relevant need from authorized context.
- **Calm:** It gives information room and does not compete for attention.
- **Credible:** It distinguishes fact, uncertainty, plan, action, and outcome.
- **Human:** It respects relationships without impersonating a person.
- **Useful:** Every visible element should support understanding or action.
- **Precise:** Status, consequence, labels, and boundaries are unambiguous.
- **Restrained:** Intelligence is felt in the experience rather than advertised through spectacle.
- **Adaptable:** One coherent system can respect different businesses, industries, languages, and accessibility needs.

JOHAI is not flashy, futuristic for its own sake, corporate-cold, playful during risk, densely analytical, anthropomorphic, or visually manipulative.

## Brand hierarchy

The brand appears differently by platform layer:

- **Public Website:** JOHAI may lead, while claims remain evidenced and current.
- **Business Workspace:** JOHAI supports the owner's operating identity and decisions; it does not act like the executive.
- **Customer Portal:** The business brand leads. JOHAI remains subordinate and must never obscure who is serving the customer.
- **AI Layer:** AI authorship is disclosed when material, but model or orchestration branding does not become the experience.
- **JOHAI Super Admin:** Operational clarity and safety take precedence over promotional expression.
- **Developer Platform:** Contracts, examples, errors, and status are precise, stable, and technically honest.

White-labeling or business branding never removes required AI, data-use, legal, security, privacy, or authorship disclosure.

## Visual identity

JOHAI's visual identity should communicate order, depth, continuity, and quiet confidence.

### Principles

- Establish one unmistakable reading order.
- Use whitespace and hierarchy before borders, cards, shadows, or decoration.
- Let content density reflect the decision, not the amount of available data.
- Make state and action more visually prominent than platform branding.
- Use visual depth to explain relationship or focus, not to simulate luxury.
- Keep AI-specific treatment subordinate to the task and consistent with non-AI states.

### Avoid

- generic “AI” gradients, glowing brains, robot imagery, data streams, sparkles, or science-fiction dashboards;
- glass effects, deep nesting, ornamental cards, and visual noise without information purpose;
- visual treatment that makes a recommendation look authoritative merely because AI produced it; and
- premium styling that reduces contrast, legibility, focus visibility, or recovery clarity.

## Mark and naming philosophy

The word `JOHAI` is direct and stable. Product or AI Employee names should describe a role or outcome rather than manufacture a personality. Do not create names that imply credentials, humanity, consciousness, ownership, or authority the system does not possess.

Any future logo, lockup, co-branding, minimum-size, exclusion-zone, and misuse specification requires a reviewed asset package. Until then, documentation must not invent exact geometry or claim an approved visual mark beyond assets actually present.

In a business-branded customer experience, the business name and identity remain primary. JOHAI attribution should be proportionate, accessible, and never deceptive.

## Typography philosophy

Typography should make complex operational information feel understandable.

- Choose highly legible typefaces with strong character distinction and complete language support.
- Use a small, repeatable hierarchy based on meaning: page purpose, section, object, status, supporting detail, and action.
- Prefer sentence case. Reserve all caps for conventional short labels only when readability remains strong.
- Use weight, size, and spacing together; never depend on weight or color alone.
- Keep line length, line height, paragraph width, and data alignment comfortable across zoom and reflow.
- Use tabular numerals only when comparison benefits and the font supports them clearly.
- Avoid novelty fonts, ultra-light body text, excessive tracking, and condensed text for critical content.

A future type scale must be tokenized, responsive, tested at 200% zoom and text-spacing overrides, and validated for every supported script.

## Spacing philosophy

Spacing is the first tool for grouping, hierarchy, and calm.

- Use a consistent base rhythm and semantic spacing tokens.
- Place related label, value, status, and action closer than unrelated groups.
- Prefer fewer meaningful groups over many boxed regions.
- Preserve touch-target separation and keyboard-focus visibility.
- Increase spacing for consequence and comprehension, not simply for visual drama.
- Allow dense operational views only when scanning and comparison are the primary task, with accessible alternatives where required.

Responsive design may change composition but must preserve meaning, order, ownership, and the primary action.

## Color philosophy

Color should communicate identity and state without becoming the only carrier of meaning.

- Use a restrained brand palette and a complete semantic palette for information, success, attention, warning, danger, selection, disabled, and focus states.
- Reserve urgent color for genuinely urgent or consequential information.
- Ensure text, icons, controls, focus indicators, charts, and adjacent states meet applicable contrast requirements.
- Pair color with text, shape, icon, position, or pattern as appropriate.
- Validate light, dark, high-contrast, forced-colors, print, and business-brand combinations before describing them as supported.
- Do not let tenant branding override safety colors, focus visibility, readable contrast, or required disclosure.

Exact approved color values belong in a separately reviewed token specification. This document intentionally does not invent them.

## Icon philosophy

Icons should clarify familiar actions, objects, and state.

- Use one coherent family with consistent stroke, optical size, and semantic meaning.
- Pair unfamiliar or consequential icons with visible text.
- Give icon-only controls an accessible name and sufficiently large target.
- Do not use ambiguous sparkle, magic, robot, or brain icons as a generic substitute for explaining AI behavior.
- Never use the same icon for materially different states.
- Mirror directional icons correctly for right-to-left languages where meaning requires it.

Decorative icons are removed from assistive technology. Meaningful icons expose an equivalent label.

## Illustration philosophy

Illustration may explain a concept, soften an empty state, or express the brand, but it must not distract from the task or invent product behavior.

- Prefer clear systems, relationships, and human-scale work over futuristic automation imagery.
- Represent a range of businesses, people, bodies, abilities, ages, cultures, and environments without tokenism.
- Avoid stereotypes tied to industry, profession, identity, emotion, or economic status.
- Do not portray AI as a person, colleague, mind, doctor, lawyer, accountant, or autonomous executive.
- Do not imply a workflow, integration, or outcome that is not available.

Critical instructions and status never depend on illustration.

## Photography philosophy

Photography should show credible human work, service, care, judgment, and customer relationships.

- Prefer specific, natural moments over staged celebration of technology.
- Preserve consent, licensing, privacy, dignity, and accurate context.
- Avoid screens full of fabricated data, glowing automation metaphors, and images that suggest professional endorsement without permission.
- Do not use real customer or employee images as synthetic test or prompt data without explicit governed authorization.
- Caption material context and provide meaningful alternative text when the image conveys information.

Stock imagery must still satisfy these rules. Visual polish does not repair an inauthentic or unsafe claim.

## Motion philosophy

Motion explains change, preserves spatial continuity, and confirms cause and effect. It does not demand attention.

- Use short, predictable transitions tied to state change.
- Keep interaction available without waiting for decorative motion.
- Preserve focus and reading position through transitions.
- Suppress or replace non-essential motion under reduced-motion preferences.
- Avoid auto-playing, looping, parallax, flashing, zooming, or large movement that creates discomfort or distraction.
- Never use motion to pressure a decision, obscure a consequence, or make AI appear active when nothing authoritative is happening.

Loading animation communicates waiting only; it never implies progress percentage or completion unless the system knows it.

## Animation philosophy

Animation is a more expressive form of motion and therefore requires a stronger purpose. Use it only for explanation, onboarding that the person requested, or brand storytelling outside a critical task.

Every animation requires:

- a documented purpose;
- accessible pause, stop, hide, and reduced-motion behavior where applicable;
- performance and battery consideration;
- no conflict with reading, input, focus, or screen-reader state; and
- an equivalent static experience.

Decorative AI character animation, simulated typing that delays content, and fake autonomous activity are prohibited.

## Micro-interactions

Micro-interactions should close the loop between action and state.

- Show immediate input acknowledgement without claiming remote completion.
- Prevent accidental duplicates and preserve user work.
- Make hover supplementary; keyboard, touch, and screen-reader users receive the same meaning.
- Keep validation specific, local, and actionable.
- Make undo available when safe and meaningful.
- Announce asynchronous success, error, and status changes accessibly.
- Avoid reward loops, confetti, streaks, artificial scarcity, and engagement mechanics unrelated to the person's goal.

The best micro-interaction is often a quiet state change with exact language.

## Accessibility philosophy

Accessibility is a constitutional requirement and a quality condition, not a visual variant.

Brand expression must preserve:

- semantic structure and logical reading order;
- complete keyboard operation and visible focus;
- accessible names, instructions, errors, and status announcements;
- contrast, text reflow, zoom, touch targets, and non-color meaning;
- reduced motion and protection from flashing or vestibular triggers;
- screen-reader, speech-input, magnification, forced-colors, and high-contrast use;
- localization expansion and right-to-left composition where supported; and
- equivalent access through loading, empty, error, denied, expired, and recovery states.

Tenant customization that fails accessibility must be corrected, constrained, or rejected. Brand consistency never outweighs access.

## Future design language

JOHAI's future design language should become more contextual and less interface-heavy while remaining explicit wherever consequence, authority, data use, AI authorship, or uncertainty matters.

It should evolve toward:

- one shared token and component foundation across platform layers;
- business branding expressed through constrained, accessible configuration;
- semantic patterns for facts, recommendations, drafts, approvals, actions, and outcomes;
- adaptive density and hierarchy based on task rather than device alone;
- optional AI that recedes behind prepared workflows;
- consistent interaction contracts across industry modules; and
- evidence-backed personalization that never widens permission.

This is a direction, not evidence that a complete cross-platform design system exists.

## Brand review checklist

Before approving a visual or interaction direction, confirm:

1. The business or person's task remains primary.
2. Hierarchy communicates purpose, state, and one primary action.
3. AI does not receive visual authority it has not earned.
4. The design is truthful about capability, authorship, and status.
5. Business branding cannot weaken disclosure, safety, or accessibility.
6. Typography, spacing, color, icons, imagery, motion, and micro-interactions have a functional reason.
7. Representative responsive, accessibility, localization, failure, and degraded states are designed.
8. The proposal passes the [Constitutional Decision Framework](../constitution/DECISION_FRAMEWORK.md).

If any answer is no or unknown, the direction returns to revision.

## Current capability and aspiration boundary

These guidelines establish permanent brand architecture. They do not claim that current routes conform completely, that a final identity package exists, or that tokens, components, themes, illustrations, photography, animations, or accessibility certifications have been implemented. Each requires separate design, implementation, review, and evidence.
